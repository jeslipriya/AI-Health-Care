First Aid Guide

Essential first aid information for common emergencies and injuries.

For life-threatening emergencies, call emergency services immediately!

Call emergency services immediately!
Adult CPR:
  1. Check for responsiveness by tapping and shouting.
  2. If unresponsive, call emergency services or have someone else call.
  3. Place the person on their back on a firm surface.
  4. Expose the chest and place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest, between the nipples.
  5. Place your other hand on top and interlock fingers.
  6. Position shoulders directly over your hands, keeping arms straight.
  7. Push hard and fast at a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute, allowing full chest recoil.
  8. Push at least 2 inches (5 cm) deep.
  9. Continue until help arrives or the person shows signs of life.

Note: Rescue breaths are optional if you're not trained or uncomfortable. Hands-only CPR can still be effective.

For a conscious adult or child (over 1 year):
  1. Ask, "Are you choking?" If the person can't speak, cough, or breathe, they need help.
  2. Stand behind the person and wrap your arms around their waist.
  3. Make a fist with one hand and place it thumb-side against the middle of their abdomen, just above the navel.
  4. Grasp your fist with your other hand.
  5. Press into the abdomen with quick, upward thrusts (Heimlich maneuver).
  6. Repeat until the object is expelled or the person becomes unconscious.
If the person becomes unconscious:
  1. Carefully lower them to the ground and call emergency services.
  2. Begin CPR, starting with chest compressions.
  3. Before giving rescue breaths, look in the mouth for the object and remove it if visible.
  4. Continue CPR until help arrives or the person revives.

  2. If possible, elevate the injured area above the level of the heart.
  3. Continue applying pressure. If blood soaks through, add more material without removing the first layer.
  4. Once bleeding is controlled, secure the dressing with a bandage or clean cloth.
  5. Seek medical attention.

For life-threatening bleeding: Call emergency services immediately. If the bleeding is from a limb and can't be controlled with direct pressure, a tourniquet might be necessary as a last resort.

First-Degree Burns (red, painful, no blisters):
  1. Cool the burn under cool (not cold) running water for 10-15 minutes.
  2. Don't use ice, as it can damage tissue.
  3. After cooling, apply a gentle moisturizer or aloe vera gel.
  4. Take over-the-counter pain relievers if needed.
Second-Degree Burns (blisters, very red, painful):
  1. Cool the burn under cool running water for 15-20 minutes.
  2. Don't break blisters.
  3. Cover loosely with a sterile bandage or clean cloth.
  4. Seek medical attention if the burn is larger than 3 inches or on the face, hands, feet, genitals, or over a joint.

For Third-Degree Burns (charred or white skin, possibly painless):

  • Call emergency services immediately.
  • Don't remove burned clothing stuck to the skin.
  • Cover the area with a clean, dry cloth until help arrives.
  • Don't immerse large severe burns in water.

Signs include pain, swelling, deformity, bruising, and inability to use the affected limb.

  1. If the injury is severe or you suspect a broken back, neck, hip, or pelvis, call emergency services. Don't move the person.
  2. Stop any bleeding by applying pressure with a clean cloth.
  3. Immobilize the injured area: Don't try to realign the bone or push a protruding bone back in.
  4. Apply ice packs wrapped in a towel to reduce swelling and pain. Don't apply directly to the skin.
  5. Treat for shock if necessary by lying the person flat with feet elevated about 12 inches (if no head, neck, or back injury is suspected).

Important: If the broken bone has punctured the skin (open fracture), it's a medical emergency. Cover the wound with a clean dressing and call emergency services immediately.

Call emergency services immediately if you suspect a heart attack!
Signs and symptoms include:
  • Chest pain, pressure, or squeezing sensation
  • Pain that radiates to the arm, jaw, neck, or back
  • Shortness of breath
  • Cold sweat, nausea, or lightheadedness
  • Women may experience less typical symptoms: fatigue, shortness of breath, nausea, back or jaw pain
While waiting for emergency services:
  1. Have the person sit or lie down in a comfortable position.
  2. Loosen any tight clothing.
  3. If the person is not allergic to aspirin and has no recent bleeding, they may chew and swallow an aspirin (if advised by emergency services).
  4. If the person stops breathing or loses consciousness, begin CPR immediately.

Call emergency services immediately if you suspect a stroke!
Remember the acronym FAST:
  • Face: Ask the person to smile. Does one side of the face droop?
  • Arms: Ask the person to raise both arms. Does one arm drift downward?
  • Speech: Ask the person to repeat a simple phrase. Is the speech slurred or strange?
  • Time: If you observe any of these signs, call emergency services immediately.
Other symptoms may include:
  • Sudden numbness or weakness, especially on one side of the body
  • Sudden confusion or trouble understanding
  • Sudden trouble seeing
  • Sudden trouble walking, dizziness, loss of balance
  • Sudden severe headache with no known cause
While waiting for emergency services:
  1. Have the person lie down with their head slightly elevated.
  2. Note the time when symptoms first appeared (important for treatment decisions).
  3. Do not give the person anything to eat or drink.
  4. If the person is unconscious, check for breathing and pulse. Begin CPR if necessary.
First Aid Kit Essentials

Every home and vehicle should have a well-stocked first aid kit. Here's what to include:

Adhesive bandages (various sizes)
Sterile gauze pads and rolls
Adhesive tape
Elastic bandages
Antiseptic wipes
Antibiotic ointment
Hydrocortisone cream
Pain relievers (acetaminophen, ibuprofen)
Scissors and tweezers
Disposable gloves
CPR breathing barrier
Emergency contact information
Get Trained

While this guide provides basic information, proper training is invaluable in emergencies.

Consider taking a certified first aid and CPR course from organizations like:

  • Red Cross
  • American Heart Association
  • St. John Ambulance
  • Local hospitals and community centers
Important Disclaimer
